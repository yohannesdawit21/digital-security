Passwords

Simple guide

Keep your accounts safe with strong passwords and smart habits

Your passwords protect email, social media, bank accounts, and other important accounts. If one weak password is stolen, attackers can get into everything.

Watch short YouTube intro

Social Engineering Attacks

What is it? Tricking people into giving passwords or private information.

Instead of hacking systems directly, attackers hack people.

Phishing: fake emails or messages
Vishing: scam phone calls
Smishing: fake SMS messages
Pretexting: fake identity story
Baiting: trap with something attractive
Urgent messages: "click now" scams

How to protect yourself

  • Never share passwords or OTP codes
  • Don’t click unknown links
  • Verify before trusting messages or calls
  • Watch for urgent or too-good-to-be-true messages

Create and Maintain Strong Passwords

Weak password examples: 123456, password, your name

Strong password example: Tg#9Lx!72@qP

  • Use at least 8 to 12 characters
  • Mix letters, numbers, and symbols
  • Do not use personal info like names or birthdays
Strong password = harder to hack.

Manage Your Passwords Safely

Common mistake: using the same password everywhere.

Why dangerous: if one account is hacked, all accounts can be hacked.

  • Use different passwords for each account
  • Use a password manager
  • Do not write passwords in unsafe places

Best practice

One account should not unlock all the others. A password manager helps create, store, and fill strong unique passwords.

Use Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

What is 2FA? An extra layer of security.

After your password, you need a second code from your phone or authenticator app.

  • Login with your password
  • Enter the code from your phone
  • Keep backup codes safe and separate
Even if a password is stolen, the attacker still can’t log in easily.

Related Tools

  • Password Managers: store and generate strong passwords
  • Authenticator Apps: generate secure login codes for 2FA
  • Antivirus Software: protect against malware stealing passwords
  • Secure Browsers: warn about fake websites

Final Important Tip

Most password hacks happen because of weak passwords, password reuse, and falling for phishing.

Stay safe by thinking before clicking, never trusting blindly, and using strong security habits.
